People ask me what I actually do all day. The honest answer is "less than you'd expect, and more on the things that matter." Here's a representative weekday at TrainYourAgent in May 2026 — the tools, the time blocks, the rituals.

This is not a productivity manifesto. It's a snapshot of what's working right now for an operator running a ~$20K MRR voice-AI shop with no team. Some of it will be obsolete by Q4. Some of it will compound for years.

## 6:15 AM — Coffee and the inbox triage

I'm up before the kid. First 15 minutes is coffee + Superhuman, processing overnight email. Not replying — sorting. Three buckets:

- **Customer issues** — anything that says "agent didn't book," "agent said something weird," "billing." These get a 90-second reply with an ETA, then go on the work list.
- **Sales / inbound** — anyone who booked a Cal.com slot or replied to a sequence. Calendly reschedule links, prep notes.
- **Everything else** — newsletter signups, Stripe receipts, Vercel deploy emails. Archive batch.

Inbox at zero by 6:30 every morning, six days a week. The rule is process, not respond. Responses happen in batches during the day.

## 7:00 AM — The dashboard check

Two browser tabs, every morning:

- **Sentry + PostHog** — any alerts on production agents from the overnight. ~3-4 days a week there's nothing. The other days it's usually a single agent that needed a prompt tweak.
- **Stripe + Supabase admin** — MRR check, new signups, churn. 30 seconds.

If a customer's agent had a bad call overnight, I listen to the recording and fix the prompt before the customer ever notices. This is the single highest-leverage thing I do all day. Customers who tell me "your agent answered every call perfectly" are customers I never lose.

## 7:30 AM – 11:30 AM — Build block

Four hours, phone in another room, Slack closed, IDE open. This is where actual product gets built. Current rotation looks like:

- Mondays — new customer build kickoffs (~half the day)
- Tuesdays — feature work on the platform (`/start` flow, new vertical templates, etc.)
- Wednesdays — content / blog / SEO (this post was a Wednesday)
- Thursdays — customer iterations (prompt tuning, integrations, edge cases)
- Fridays — refactor + cleanup + automation work

Tools in this block:
- **VS Code + Cursor** — primary IDE. Cursor's tab-complete is too good to pass on.
- **Claude Code in agent mode** — for any task longer than 20 minutes
- **GitHub PRs** — even solo, every change is a PR with a self-review
- **Vercel + Supabase + Fly.io** — the deploy targets
- **Linear** — single-engineer Kanban, mostly to keep "things I forgot" out of my head

## 11:30 AM — Sales call #1

I take one or two sales calls a day, never more. Calls are 30 min on Cal.com. Format:

- 5 min: their context (what they do, current phone setup, biggest pain)
- 10 min: live demo against their actual scenario
- 10 min: scope + price
- 5 min: next step (build kickoff or follow-up)

I close ~40-50% of qualified calls. The qualifying happens before the call (pathway routing, form questions, my own LinkedIn check). Unqualified people don't get on the calendar.

Tools:
- **Cal.com** for booking
- **Fathom** for transcript + summary (auto-posts to my CRM)
- **HubSpot Free** as the CRM
- **DocuSign** if it's a same-day close (rare but happens)

## 12:30 PM — Lunch and the longest walk of the day

90 minutes, no laptop. Walk + lunch + family time. The hardest discipline I've built. Used to skip it. Skipping made every afternoon worse and every Wednesday I'd realize I hadn't talked to my wife in three days. Now it's a hard block.

I do read on the walk — usually one essay from a builder I respect. Currently going through old David Cain, Patrick McKenzie, Brian Lui posts. Operator-brain food.

## 2:00 PM – 5:00 PM — Customer block

This is the part of the day that pays the bills. Three hours of customer work:

- Prompt tuning based on yesterday's call recordings
- Integration work (Hubspot, ServiceTitan, Cal.com webhooks)
- Customer Loom recordings (5-10 min, sent same-day, showing them what changed)
- Slack replies in their channels

A typical afternoon I'll send 4-6 Looms. Customers eat them up. The Loom replaces the "weekly check-in call" everyone else's vendor schedules. They feel cared for; I don't lose a 30-min slot of calendar.

Tools:
- **Loom** (obviously)
- **Slack Connect** for active customer channels
- **Postman** for hitting their APIs
- **Bland / Vapi / our self-hosted Pipecat** depending on which platform the customer is on

## 5:00 PM — Sales call #2 (sometimes)

If there's a second sales call it sits here. Same format, same close rate. After 5:30 the day is done for paid work.

## 5:30 PM – 8:00 PM — Family time

Hard block. Phone face-down. Watch is off. The work will be there tomorrow.

## 8:00 PM – 9:30 PM — Build-in-public hour

This is the hour I write, ship public artifacts, or work on the parts of the business that don't have an immediate dollar attached. Recently:

- Blog posts (this one)
- Twitter / LinkedIn — one substantial post per platform per day, mostly the same content
- Programmatic SEO experiments
- Newsletter prep — TrainYourAgent ships weekly on Wednesday morning

It's not glamorous. It's the compound-interest hour. Almost nothing I do here pays off in the same week. Most of what I do here pays off 90 days out, and pays off again 18 months out.

## The tools, in one list

For the people who skipped to the bottom:

- **Cursor + Claude Code** — IDE + agent coding
- **Linear** — single-eng kanban
- **GitHub** — every change in a PR
- **Vercel + Supabase + Fly.io** — production
- **Sentry + PostHog** — observability
- **Superhuman** — email
- **Cal.com + Fathom** — sales calls
- **HubSpot Free + Stripe** — CRM + billing
- **Loom + Slack Connect** — customer comms
- **Notion + Apple Notes** — anything that needs to be written down
- **beehiiv** — newsletter

## What I had to stop doing

Three things I stopped, in order of how much time they returned:

1. **Group chats with other founders.** Slack DMs with friends-of-friends in similar businesses ate 90 min/day. The signal-to-noise was bad. I'm in two Discords now, both with hard mute on every channel except announcements.
2. **Looking at metrics more than twice a day.** Stripe MRR, web traffic, signups — checking these is dopamine, not work. Twice a day. Morning, after lunch. That's it.
3. **Saying yes to "quick podcast appearances."** A 30-min podcast is a 90-min commitment by the time you account for prep, recording, the social post, the follow-up. I do one a month if it has the right audience. Three years ago I'd do six.

## The compounding rituals

Three things I started, in order of how much they paid back:

1. **Every customer call recording listened to within 24h.** Catches problems before they're complaints.
2. **One Loom per week to every active customer, even if there's nothing to report.** Single biggest retention lever I have.
3. **A weekly Friday "what compounded this week" note in Notion.** 10 minutes. Forces me to notice the small wins that would otherwise blur.

That's the loom of the day. Boring, mostly. Repeatable, mostly. Compounding, hopefully.
