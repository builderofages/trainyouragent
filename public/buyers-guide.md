## The five things that actually matter

### 1. Trained on YOUR data, not a generic prompt
A real agent ingests your scripts, pricing, FAQs, ticket history, and product docs. A demo built on a system prompt isn't training — it's a wrapper.

**How to vet:** Ask "what data sources is the agent trained on?" If the answer is "we use the latest LLMs," it's a wrapper. If the answer involves your CRM, your ticket history, your transcripts — that's training.

### 2. Native integrations to YOUR stack
Booking should write into the calendar you actually use. Tickets should land in the CRM you actually use. Not webhooks duct-taped together.

**How to vet:** Name the three tools you use most. If the vendor says "yes, we integrate via Zapier," that's duct tape. If they name the integration product they've built, ask to see it live.

### 3. Multi-model fallback
Anthropic, OpenAI, Google all have outages. A serious vendor routes between them. A weekend vendor goes down when their primary model has a bad afternoon.

**How to vet:** Ask "what happens if your LLM provider has an outage?" If they pause, the answer is "you go down."

### 4. Real production observability
You should see every call's transcript, cost, quality score, and outcome in a dashboard. Not "we send you a weekly email."

**How to vet:** Ask for a screenshot of the dashboard a current customer sees. If they can't show you, the dashboard doesn't exist.

### 5. Tuning, not just deployment
The agent on Day 1 is not the agent on Day 90. Real vendors have a tuning process — sampling calls weekly, updating prompts, rolling back changes that hurt outcomes.

**How to vet:** Ask "who at your company will tune my agent and how often?" Vague answers = no process.

## The seven questions vendors dodge

1. **What's your average per-call cost (not per-minute)?** Per-minute pricing without per-call context is meaningless.
2. **Show me a recording of one of your live agent calls.** Not a demo — a real call from a real customer.
3. **What's your SLA on uptime, and how do you compensate when you breach it?** No SLA = no skin in the game.
4. **Who owns the data on cancel day?** Anything other than "you do, and we delete our copy in 30 days" is a red flag.
5. **Do you train AI models on customer data?** If yes, walk away.
6. **What's the largest agent you've shipped to production?** Vague answers = small portfolio.
7. **Can I talk to one of your customers?** If they refuse, the customers don't exist or aren't happy.

## Pricing reality

The cheapest per-minute on the market in 2026 is around $0.05-0.08. The most expensive is $0.50+. The right number for you depends on conversion: a $0.05 agent that books 20% of qualifying calls costs you more per booking than a $0.20 agent that books 55%. Always ask for cost-per-outcome, not cost-per-minute.

A reasonable build fee in 2026 is $3,500-$10,000 depending on integration complexity. Anything under $1,500 is an off-the-shelf wrapper. Anything over $25,000 should come with named senior engineers and a multi-month delivery plan.
