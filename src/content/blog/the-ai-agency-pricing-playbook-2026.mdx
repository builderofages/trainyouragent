I ran a Los Angeles social media marketing agency for three years before TrainYourAgent. I tried every pricing model on the planet — performance fees, equity slices, day rates, hourly, fixed scope, monthly retainer, revenue share. The hard-won lesson: **the pricing model decides what kind of work you do**, more than the work decides what pricing model you should use.

Now I'm at ~$20K MRR on TrainYourAgent — voice and chat agents for SMB. Here's the actual playbook I use to scope and price deals in 2026. Real numbers, real scripts, real margins.

## The four pricing models that work for AI agencies

There are exactly four pricing structures that survive contact with real customers:

1. **Fixed-fee monthly retainer** ($799 / $1,499 / $2,500 / $5,000 / mo tiers)
2. **Project + retainer** ($X build fee + $Y/mo to maintain)
3. **Performance** ($X per booked appointment, $Y per converted lead)
4. **Equity / rev-share** (you take a slice of the customer instead of cash)

Everything else — hourly, daily, "we'll figure it out" — fails at SMB scale. Hourly punishes you for being efficient. Daily makes you a contractor. "We'll figure it out" never gets paid.

## When fixed-fee wins (most of the time)

Fixed monthly fee is the default for ~80% of our customers. The math is clean for both sides: they know their bill, you know your MRR, the conversation stays on outcomes.

The tiering we use:

- **$799/mo Operators** — single agent, single channel, 3,000 minutes or 5,000 chats included
- **$1,499/mo Operators+** — multi-channel, 8,000 min / 15,000 chats, CRM sync
- **$2,500/mo Scale** — multi-agent, multi-language, dedicated build engineer
- **$5,000+/mo Custom** — multi-location SMB or multi-product DTC; scoped per deal

Each tier has a **build fee** on top — $2,950 to $9,950 depending on scope — which front-loads the engineering work and filters tire-kickers.

Why fixed-fee works at SMB scale:

- The customer's biggest fear is "surprise bill in month two." Fixed fee removes it.
- You can model MRR cleanly for runway planning.
- The pricing conversation is 2 minutes, not 30. They either fit a tier or they don't.

The tradeoff: a customer doing 8x average volume on the cheapest tier is dragging your margin. You need usage caps with overage rates ("up to 3,000 min, $0.18/min after"). Without that, fixed-fee will eat you alive in the long run.

## When project + retainer wins (custom builds)

For custom work — multi-location chains, complex integrations, agency white-label setups — split the deal into:

- **Build fee**, paid 50% on signing / 50% on go-live: $6K – $24K typical
- **Ongoing retainer**, monthly: $1,500 – $4,500/mo for maintenance + iteration

This is what I use for clients who want something we don't ship as a standard product. The build fee covers actual engineering hours (figure $200–$300/hour loaded). The retainer covers monitoring, model updates, prompt tuning, and ~4 hours of iteration per month.

The script that closes these:

> "Our typical custom build is $9,950 — 50% on signing, 50% on go-live in ~30 days. After that, we maintain it for $2,500/mo, which includes the on-call rotation and 4 hours of iteration per month. That's the floor. If your scope is bigger we'll quote it before signing."

Concrete, bounded, no ambiguity. Closes 60-70% of qualified leads. Tire-kickers self-eject when they hear "50% on signing."

## When performance pricing wins (almost never, but when it does)

Performance pricing — "$50 per booked appointment, $200 per closed deal" — sounds great on paper. It aligns your incentives with the customer's. In practice it almost never works at SMB scale.

Why:
- Attribution is messy (was it the agent or their existing ad spend?)
- Customers dispute every "qualified" lead
- You front-load all the build cost, then wait months for outcomes
- Cash flow is brutal

The ONLY time I'll do performance:
- High-volume vertical (real estate, solar, mortgage)
- Customer has clean attribution already in place (HubSpot or ServiceTitan tracking)
- $250+ payout per qualified outcome (so unit economics survive variance)
- 6-month minimum commitment with $X/mo floor to cover infrastructure

Even then, I price it as a **hybrid**: $1,500/mo floor + $75 per booked appointment over baseline. The floor protects you. The per-appointment kicker rewards both sides for success.

## When equity / rev-share makes sense (rarely, with eyes open)

Equity in a customer is a real option if all three are true:

1. The customer is fundraising or about to (real equity, not napkin-equity)
2. You'd be ~3-5% of the total work this year (any more and you should be co-founder)
3. The cash deal would be in the same ballpark anyway

I've taken equity twice. One went to zero. One is currently worth ~6x what the cash would have been. Expected value is positive in retrospect, but the cash compounds in your business RIGHT NOW. Equity compounds in someone else's company at someone else's pace.

Rev-share is similar but cleaner — you take 5-10% of revenue attributable to the agent for 12-24 months. Easier to track than equity, harder to argue about than performance. I've done two rev-share deals; both worked. The trick is **time-box it**. Open-ended rev-share is a rope.

## The scripts that move pricing conversations forward

Pricing conversations stall in the same three places. Here's what to say.

**When they ask "how much?" in the first email:**

> "It depends on volume and integrations, but our SMB packages start at $799/mo + $2,950 to build. Custom builds run $6K–$24K. If you've got 10 minutes I can scope your specific setup on a quick call — that's the fastest way to a real number."

You're not being cagey. You're framing the range and pivoting to a call. Works ~70% of the time.

**When they say "the budget is tight":**

> "Totally fair. The smallest version we ship is $799/mo flat + the build fee. If that's still high, what we usually do is start with one channel — voice OR chat, not both — and add the second in month 3 once you're seeing returns. Want me to scope that?"

Don't discount. Down-scope. Discounting trains them to negotiate. Down-scoping trains them to expand later.

**When they say "let me think about it":**

> "Of course. What's the open question? If it's pricing, we can scope smaller. If it's technical fit, I can show you the same setup running for [similar customer]. If it's timing, we can start the build in 30 days. Which is it?"

This works because it asks them to be specific. ~40% of the time they tell you the real objection and you solve it in the next two sentences.

## What I'd never do again

Three pricing mistakes I made in the SMMA days, in case you're tempted:

1. **Year-long contracts at a discount.** They sound like security. They become resentment. Month-to-month with a 30-day notice clause closes faster and has higher retention because customers stay by choice.
2. **Bundling unrelated services.** Voice agent + website + SEO + ads as one bundle. The customer churns the bundle when one piece underperforms. Sell distinct services. Cross-sell after they're a customer.
3. **Custom proposals for sub-$2K/mo deals.** Below $2K/mo the proposal is the deal-killer — too much process for the customer's brain to process. Below $2K, the conversation is: "here's the tier, here's the link, you in?"

## The 2026 reality

AI agencies are scaling faster than ever right now because the marginal cost of an extra customer is near zero. The constraint isn't engineering — it's whether you can hold pricing.

The shops that race to the bottom on price are getting margin-compressed inside 90 days. The shops that hold $799+/mo with a real build fee are stacking MRR at a rate that compounds. Same engineering effort, very different outcomes.

Pick a tier. Hold it. Down-scope before discounting. Front-load with build fees. Don't take performance deals without a floor. Don't take equity unless you'd take the cash anyway.
