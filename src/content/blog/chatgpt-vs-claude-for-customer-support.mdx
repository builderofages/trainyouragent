I get asked weekly which model to use for customer support. The "which is smarter" question is the wrong one. The right one is: *which one ships the right answer in under 4 seconds, costs under $0.04 per resolved ticket, and doesn't hallucinate a refund policy that doesn't exist?* That's a different bake-off.

So we ran one. 60 real anonymized support tickets from three TrainYourAgent customers (a DTC skincare brand, a SaaS onboarding team, a roofing scheduling desk) through GPT-5 and Claude Sonnet 4.6 with identical system prompts, identical RAG context, identical evaluator rubric. Here's what we found.

## The test setup

Three test buckets, 20 tickets each:

- **Bucket A — Policy questions**: refunds, shipping windows, return eligibility, plan upgrades/downgrades.
- **Bucket B — Multi-turn troubleshooting**: install error, billing dispute, account recovery, "this feature isn't working."
- **Bucket C — Empathy + escalation**: angry customer, urgent, billing emergency, "I want to speak to a manager."

Same system prompt for both:

```
You are a support agent for [BRAND]. Use only the provided knowledge base
to answer. If the knowledge base does not contain the answer, say so plainly
and offer to escalate. Be warm, brief (under 6 sentences when possible),
and do not invent policy. End every reply with a single concrete next step.
```

Same RAG: top-5 chunks from each brand's actual KB at retrieval temperature 0.0. Same temperature on the models: 0.3. Evaluator: a third LLM (Claude Opus 4) blind-scoring on (a) factual accuracy vs the KB, (b) appropriate empathy, (c) brevity, (d) safe escalation behavior.

## Headline result

```
Metric                  GPT-5   Claude Sonnet 4.6
─────────────────────── ─────   ─────────────────
Factual accuracy        91.7%   94.2%
Empathy (eval score)    7.8/10  8.5/10
Brevity (avg sentences) 5.3     4.4
Safe-escalate trigger   78%     91%
Hallucinated policy     7/60    2/60
Latency p50             2.1s    1.6s
Latency p95             4.8s    3.9s
Cost per 1k tickets     $14.20  $11.40
```

Claude won on every column we cared about for support — but the gaps are smaller than they look, and there are categories where GPT-5 was the better choice. Read on.

## Where GPT-5 wins

### 1. Code-heavy support

If your support tickets are 60%+ "my integration broke, here's the stack trace" — GPT-5's code-reasoning is still a half-step ahead. The SaaS bucket included 4 tickets with actual Python tracebacks. GPT-5 nailed 4/4. Claude got 3/4 and asked a clarifying question on the fourth (correct behavior, but slower resolution).

### 2. Multi-language support at low cost

GPT-5 nano (the small model variant) is the cost king for Spanish/Portuguese/French support at scale. If you're doing 50k tickets/month in a mixed-language ecommerce flow, the cost delta adds up: $14/1k tickets × 50k = $700/mo vs $570 with Claude. But GPT-5 nano at $4.20/1k tickets = $210/mo. The accuracy drop from nano was 6 points though — only acceptable for low-stakes tickets.

### 3. Strict-format JSON output

GPT-5 still has a small edge at strictly-structured JSON output with deeply nested schemas. If your support agent has to output `{intent, urgency, sentiment, suggested_action, draft_reply, escalation_target}` every single time and you're parsing it programmatically — GPT-5 had 0 schema violations across 60 tickets. Claude had 2 (both `null` where it should have used an empty string).

## Where Claude wins

### 1. Empathy on angry tickets

The bucket-C empathy delta was the biggest. Sample ticket:

> "I've been a customer for 4 years and this is the SECOND time you've billed me twice. I want my money back and I want to know why this keeps happening. This is unacceptable."

GPT-5 reply (excerpt):
> "I understand this is frustrating. Let me look into your account. I see two charges on March 15th for $89 each. I'll process a refund for the duplicate charge and it should appear in 5-7 business days. Is there anything else I can help with?"

Claude reply (excerpt):
> "I'm really sorry — being double-billed twice in your fourth year as a customer is exactly the kind of thing that breaks trust, and I don't blame you for being frustrated. I can see both charges on your account from March 15th. I'm refunding the duplicate now and you'll see it back in 5-7 business days. Separately, I'm flagging this to our billing engineering team so we can figure out the root cause — I'll personally make sure someone follows up with you within 48 hours with what we found and what we changed. If you want to escalate to a manager in the meantime, just say the word."

The Claude reply landed every emotional beat. It also did something GPT-5 didn't — proposed a root-cause follow-up. That difference shows up in CSAT.

### 2. Refusing to invent policy

Sample ticket: *"Can I get a refund? I bought the annual plan in January and want to cancel now."*

The KB explicitly states: "Annual plans are non-refundable after the 14-day satisfaction window. Pro-rata credits may be issued at customer-success discretion."

GPT-5: "I can refund you $X for the remaining months." (Invented refund.)

Claude: "Looking at your account, you're past the 14-day window, so a full refund isn't standard. What I can do is offer a pro-rata credit toward a future purchase — that's at our team's discretion and I think your situation qualifies. Want me to apply it?"

Claude got this right 18/20 times. GPT-5 got it right 13/20. The hallucinated-policy problem is the single biggest support-agent failure mode and Claude is just better at it.

### 3. Knowing when to escalate

Sample ticket: *"I think I'm having a panic attack from the stress of this dispute. Please help me."*

GPT-5: "I can help you with your dispute. Can you tell me your account email?"

Claude: "I want to make sure you're okay first. If you're feeling overwhelmed, please take care of yourself — and if you need someone to talk to immediately, you can reach the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline by calling or texting 988. I'm here to help with the dispute whenever you're ready, and I'm also looping in a human teammate so you don't have to navigate this alone."

GPT-5 missed the safety signal entirely. Claude not only caught it but escalated *and* stayed on-task for the original ask. This is rare but it's the kind of thing that, when it happens, defines whether you trust the agent in production.

## The latency surprise

We expected Claude to be slower. It wasn't. Sonnet 4.6 was about 25% faster on both p50 and p95 in our test. The reason: GPT-5's reasoning effort was being applied even on tickets where it wasn't needed, adding 0.5-1s of "thinking" latency. Claude was more aggressive about when to skip that.

If you're running an agent where every second of latency hurts (voice agents over a phone line, live chat with a 5-second visible response budget), Claude has the edge in 2026 — and that's a sentence I didn't expect to write 18 months ago.

## Cost math at real scale

For a 10k-resolution-per-month support agent (our typical SaaS customer):

- **GPT-5**: $142/mo on model calls + $40/mo on retrieval = $182/mo
- **Claude Sonnet 4.6**: $114/mo on model calls + $40/mo on retrieval = $154/mo
- **Human BPO equivalent (10k contacts at $0.85/contact)**: $8,500/mo

The delta between the two LLMs is small. The delta to humans is two orders of magnitude. The question isn't "which model" — it's "are you running an agent at all?"

## Our default recommendation in 2026

For support agents on TrainYourAgent installs:

- **Default to Claude Sonnet 4.6** — best blend of accuracy, empathy, latency, cost.
- **Use GPT-5 mini for code-heavy SaaS support** where the user pastes tracebacks routinely.
- **Use GPT-5 nano for high-volume low-stakes ecommerce** where you can absorb a small accuracy hit for a 60% cost reduction.
- **Always have a human escalation path** wired up — neither model is good enough to be the only line.

## The prompts that actually performed

Two non-obvious prompt patterns moved scores by 5+ points across both models:

**Pattern 1 — Cite-or-decline.** Add to the system prompt:
```
For every factual claim about policy, prices, or timelines, cite the
source KB chunk in brackets like [doc: refund-policy.md]. If you cannot
cite a source, refuse to make the claim.
```
This cut hallucinated-policy rate by 70% on both models.

**Pattern 2 — Single concrete next step.** Add:
```
End every reply with exactly one concrete next step in the format:
"Next: [I will / Please / We will] ..."
```
This raised resolution rate by 12% — customers replied faster when they knew exactly what to do.
