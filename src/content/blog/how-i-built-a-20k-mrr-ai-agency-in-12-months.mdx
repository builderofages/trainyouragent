I get DMs every week from founders trying to build the same thing — a custom AI agent practice serving SMBs. The number ($20K MRR) sounds small to the SaaS-poisoned founder twitter crowd, and it sounds large to the freelancer crowd. The truth is it's a meaningful milestone for the kind of business it is: services, owned-IP, recurring, profitable from month two. Here's the honest story of how I got here.

## The detour that turned out to be the moat

Before TrainYourAgent, I had five business chapters that all looked unrelated:

- **Operations engineer at a multi-site industrial company** — learned to instrument processes, write SOPs that actually got followed, and respect the difference between "looks automated" and "is automated."
- **Welder** — three years on the floor. The lesson wasn't welding. It was: customers will pay a premium for someone who shows up on time, finishes the job, and doesn't blame the materials.
- **Market trader** — futures, options, a bit of crypto when crypto was new. Learned to make decisions with incomplete information and to size positions when I was wrong. This is the single most useful skill I have as a founder.
- **LA SMMA (social-media marketing agency)** — three years running paid ads for service businesses in Southern California. Burned every offer, every funnel, every pricing model. Made $0 net for the first year. Made $14K MRR in year three.
- **EndCreations (ed-tech founder)** — built a product, raised a small round, learned what it's like to be on the other side of the investor table.

Each chapter, individually, looked like a detour. Stacked together they became the moat for TrainYourAgent. The operations background taught me to scope a build. The trader background taught me to price. The SMMA taught me how to acquire SMBs. The founder background taught me how to talk to engineering. Welding taught me to ship.

If you're a founder reading this thinking your past is unrelated to where you want to go — it isn't. The compounding only becomes obvious in retrospect.

## Month 0–3: the wrong offer

I started TrainYourAgent in May 2025 with the obvious offer: *"I'll build you a custom AI voice agent for $5,000."* This was wrong for three reasons:

1. **The price was a one-time fee**, which meant no recurring revenue, which meant I was always selling.
2. **The deliverable was a "voice agent,"** which is a feature, not an outcome. SMBs don't buy features.
3. **The pricing was a discount of a vague benchmark.** Nobody knew if $5,000 was cheap or expensive. The conversations all turned into negotiations.

In those three months I closed four deals at an average of $4,200. Total revenue: $16,800. Cost of fulfillment (my time, contractor help, infra): roughly the same. Net: $0.

## Month 4: the rewrite

I rewrote the offer at the end of August 2025. The new shape:

- **Outcome, not feature.** "We capture every after-hours call so you stop losing $40k/year in missed booking revenue."
- **Recurring, not one-time.** $1,499/mo retainer + $4,950 build fee, 6-month minimum.
- **Specific to a vertical.** I picked HVAC first. The website headline literally said "HVAC after-hours dispatch agent."

I closed three new HVAC accounts in September 2025. MRR went from $0 to $4,497 in one month. The thing that changed wasn't my skill — it was the offer's specificity.

The lesson I'd hammer into every founder reading this: **you don't have a product problem, you have an offer problem.** The product can be the same. The offer is what people decide on.

## Month 5–7: the vertical multiplier

Once the HVAC offer worked, I copy-pasted it across four more verticals: roofing, healthcare, dental, real estate. Each one got its own landing page, its own demo, its own case-study scaffold. Same engineering, same retainer price, different positioning.

By Christmas 2025 I was at $9,400 MRR across nine accounts. The work pattern was now: spend 60% of time on fulfillment, 30% on sales/marketing, 10% on building infrastructure that compounded.

The biggest single revenue multiplier in this period was the **booking-rate guarantee**. I started offering: *"If we don't lift your booking rate by 15 points in 60 days, you don't pay the retainer."* Of the four customers who took the guarantee, all four hit it. Word of mouth started.

## Month 8–10: the agency channel

Around February 2026 I added an agency partner program. Existing marketing agencies bringing me their HVAC/dental/legal clients in exchange for a 20% kickback on year-one revenue.

This was a force multiplier I underestimated. Five partner agencies brought 11 deals over two months — and partner-sourced deals had a 40% higher close rate because they came pre-warmed by a trusted advisor.

MRR crossed $14k in March 2026. I hired my first part-time engineer to handle build-out and freed myself for sales.

## Month 11–12: the leverage flip

April-May 2026: MRR crossed $20k. The composition of the business at $20k MRR:

- 14 active accounts (mix of $799, $1,499, and $2,500 tiers)
- 78% gross margin (after infra, contractor, and software costs)
- 21-day average time-to-close from first call to signed contract
- $4,200 average annual contract value if we annualize current MRR per customer
- Roughly $11k/mo retained earnings after I take a $5k draw

The biggest change at $20k was **the leverage flipped**. For most of months 4-10 I was the bottleneck — every sales call, every prompt rewrite, every onboarding. By month 11 the engineer handled 60% of fulfillment, partner agencies sourced 40% of new deals, the website and the demos were generating 30% of inbound on their own. I had time to think again.

## What I did right

- **Picked one vertical first.** HVAC was the wedge. Without it I'd have ended up with one of every kind of client and zero leverage.
- **Charged for pilots.** Even $500. It filters tire-kickers and signals you're a business, not a hobby.
- **Wrote case studies obsessively.** Every install got a one-pager I could show the next prospect. By month 8 I had 11 of them.
- **Built the website like an engineer**, not a marketer. Real demos that hit real APIs. SMBs are over-marketed-to and under-shown.
- **Ran my own ads with a small budget.** $1,500/mo on Meta and LinkedIn. The agency-channel acquisition was bigger but the ad-driven inbound was steady baseline.

## What I did wrong

- **Spent too long on the wrong offer.** I should have rewritten in month two, not month four. I cost myself ~$10k of MRR by being slow.
- **Tried to charge by the hour for one customer.** It was a disaster. Lost a month of margin. Won't do it again.
- **Underpriced the first three customers.** Two of them are still on the original cheap retainer. I'll grandfather them forever but it taught me to price for what I'd charge the *third* customer, not the *first*.
- **Built too many "trust" pages on the website too early.** Customers buy the demo, not the about page. Demo first, trust pages second.

## What I'd do differently from day one

If I started over tomorrow with what I know now:

1. **Day 1: pick one vertical and ignore the rest for 90 days.**
2. **Day 1: price at recurring retainer + build fee. No exceptions.**
3. **Day 7: ship a working demo on a dedicated landing page for that vertical.**
4. **Day 14: start a podcast or weekly video targeting that vertical's owner persona.**
5. **Day 21: send 100 cold emails per day with one offer to one persona.**
6. **Day 60: charge the first paying customer. Even at half-price.**
7. **Day 90: write the first case study. Use it on every subsequent call.**

That's the path. There's no shortcut.

## The thesis I'm running on

The bigger thing I'm betting on isn't "AI agencies." It's that the **services-to-software transition for SMB ops is just beginning** and the right business shape is the one that ships the agent, owns the IP, and earns the retainer. Pure SaaS is too cold for SMBs. Pure consulting doesn't scale. The hybrid — services-now, productize-later — is the right path for the next 24 months.

## If you're trying to do the same thing

Two things to do today:

1. Pick the vertical. Don't keep researching. Pick one, commit 90 days.
2. [Book a call](/book) if you want to talk through your specific situation. I do a few of these per week with founders who are trying to build something similar — usually free, 30 minutes, no pitch.

The hardest part of this business isn't the engineering or the sales. It's the patience to let a single offer cook for 90 days before judging it. Most founders quit at week 4. The ones who get to $20k MRR are the ones who didn't.
